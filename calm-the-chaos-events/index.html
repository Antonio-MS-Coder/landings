01

Three roles, kept separatebecause they are three different jobs

Most couples hire a planner, then a separate officiant, then discover in month nine that nobody actually owns the day itself. Calm the Chaos lists all three. Here is what each one is, and when it happens.

01 Full-service planning

The long stretch, before anyone else is involved

Planning is the months of decisions that happen well before a guest ever sees a venue: what the day is supposed to feel like, what it costs, who is hired, and in what order everything has to be locked in.

  • Shape of the day, budget, and priorities set early
  • Vendor shortlisting, bookings, and contract dates tracked
  • Floor plan, seating, and the master timeline drafted
  • One person holding the whole file, start to finish

Runs: earliest planning → the week of

02 Officiating

The twenty minutes the whole day is built around

Officiating is the ceremony itself — the part every other hour on the schedule is arranged in service of. Because Calm the Chaos also offers officiating, the person who wrote the timeline can be the person standing at the front of it.

  • The ceremony performed, start to finish
  • Processional and recessional cues built into the run of show
  • No hand-off between the planner and a vendor meeting you that morning

Runs: one fixed point on the schedule

03 Day-of coordination

Somebody has to run the actual day

Coordination is execution: holding the schedule on the day, cueing vendors, absorbing the questions that would otherwise reach the couple, and quietly rebuilding the timeline when something runs late — because something always does.

  • Vendor arrivals, load-in, and load-out sequenced
  • Every cue called against a written run of show
  • Problems intercepted before they reach the couple or the family
  • The plan adjusted live when the schedule slips

Runs: load-in → last car out
